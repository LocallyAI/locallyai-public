## Summary

Following our discussions with the client and review of the available
financial and operational data, we recommend a phased restructuring
proceeding under Articles 17–35 of the Bankruptcy Law of the Kingdom
of Saudi Arabia (Royal Decree M/50, 1439H) — protective settlement

non-Saudi framework. This memo sets out the rationale, the timeline,
and the principal workstreams.

A prior assessment by the client's auditors identified [N] potential

We have ruled out (4) on going-concern grounds; (3) is not available

## Rationale for protective settlement proceedings

### Stay of creditor enforcement

Once filed, the protective settlement proceedings impose a moratorium
on enforcement actions by creditors (Bankruptcy Law Art. 22). This
gives the client breathing room to negotiate terms without parallel
enforcement risk.

### Court oversight

The procedure is supervised by the competent Saudi commercial court,
which provides creditors confidence in the process and enables a
binding outcome under Bankruptcy Law Art. 33.

### Continuity of operations

Unlike liquidation proceedings, the protective settlement procedure
contemplates the debtor's continued trading. Management remains in
place subject to the supervision of a court-appointed trustee.

### Familiarity to the senior creditor consortium

The senior creditors include both Saudi banks and international
lenders with KSA exposure. The protective settlement framework is
recognised by all of them.

## Workstreams

We recommend the following parallel workstreams over an eight-week
preparation horizon:

### 1. Legal preparation (lead: this firm)
- Draft the application to the competent court including the
  proposed protective settlement plan and the supporting financial
  evidence.
- Engage with the client's auditors to certify the going-concern
  basis required by Art. 18.
- Brief the proposed trustee.

### 2. Financial advisory (lead: client's investment bank)
- Build the recovery scenarios (high / medium / low) for each
  creditor class.
- Prepare the cashflow-projection package for the application.
- Negotiate the term sheet with the senior creditor consortium.

### 3. Stakeholder management (lead: client management)
- Communications plan for employees, suppliers, key customers.
- Regulatory notifications: CMA (if listed), SAMA (if banking
  exposures), tax authority (ZATCA) for going-concern impact on
  current-year tax assessment.

### 4. Personal data — PDPL implications
The protective settlement filing requires disclosure of:
- the client's senior management roles and remuneration;
- significant suppliers and customers (commercial Personal Data
  insofar as natural-person counterparties);
- pending litigation involving identifiable individuals;
- a list of senior creditors (commercial entities, generally not
  Personal Data, but care needed where individuals are creditors).

The court file is, in principle, public on the protective-
settlement docket. Where the client's PDPL obligations and the
court-disclosure obligations conflict, court-disclosure prevails;
the disclosed Personal Data should be the minimum necessary.

We will prepare a redacted exhibit-bundle methodology with the
client and brief the court on PDPL considerations as part of the
application.

## Risks and mitigations

### Risk 1 — leak of restructuring during preparation
**Mitigation.** Strict need-to-know circulation. NDAs in place with
each adviser. The client should identify the minimal set of
internal personnel briefed and update the rest only at filing.

### Risk 2 — challenger creditor blocks the application
**Mitigation.** Pre-filing engagement with the senior creditor
consortium gives them a veto in practice; we recommend not filing
without their term-sheet endorsement.

### Risk 3 — operational disruption on filing date
**Mitigation.** Communications plan rehearsed in advance;
"day-one" letters drafted for employees, key customers, key
suppliers; treasury arrangements adjusted before filing so
post-filing payroll and critical-supplier payments continue
seamlessly.

### Risk 4 — regulatory exposure
**Mitigation.** Pre-filing engagement with relevant regulators
(CMA, ZATCA) reduces the risk of surprise interventions
post-filing.

### Risk 5 — challenge from the trustee on management performance
**Mitigation.** The protective-settlement framework keeps management
in place but under trustee supervision. Maintaining transparent
records and full co-operation with the trustee minimises this
risk.
