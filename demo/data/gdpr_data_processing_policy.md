# Data Protection and UK GDPR Processing Policy

**Document type:** Internal firm policy
**Owner:** Data Protection Officer (DPO)
**Review cadence:** Annual

This policy sets out how the firm handles personal data in compliance with the UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) and the Data Protection Act 2018 ("DPA 2018"). It applies to all partners, fee earners, support staff, contractors, and third-party processors acting on the firm's behalf.

## 1. Lawful Bases for Processing

The firm relies on the following lawful bases under Article 6(1) UK GDPR:

- **Performance of a contract** — when processing client data to deliver legal services pursuant to a signed engagement letter.
- **Legal obligation** — anti-money-laundering, professional conduct, court orders, regulatory reporting.
- **Legitimate interests** — limited internal administration (e.g. business-development analytics) where the interest is not overridden by data-subject rights.
- **Consent** — only used for marketing communications and cookies, never as a primary basis for client-matter processing.

Special category data (Article 9) — including health, criminal-conviction, and biometric data — is processed only where Article 9(2) conditions are met, typically (b) employment law, (f) legal claims, or (g) substantial public interest.

## 2. Data Subject Rights

The firm honours all data-subject rights under Articles 12–22 UK GDPR. Requests are routed to the DPO at dpo@firm.example and acknowledged within 72 hours. The firm responds substantively within one calendar month, extendable by a further two months for complex or numerous requests with notice to the data subject.

Rights administered:

- **Access** (Article 15) — disclosure subject to legal-professional-privilege carve-outs.
- **Rectification** (Article 16).
- **Erasure** (Article 17) — balanced against retention obligations under SRA, AML, and litigation hold rules.
- **Restriction** (Article 18).
- **Portability** (Article 20).
- **Objection** (Article 21).

Privilege and confidentiality obligations to other clients may legitimately limit disclosures; the DPO documents the analysis on the matter file.

## 3. Data Minimisation

The firm collects only the personal data strictly necessary for the matter. Identification documents are retained per AML rules (5 years from end of business relationship by default). Internal management information uses pseudonymised identifiers wherever feasible — for example, audit logs in case-management systems record salted-hash user identifiers rather than names.

## 4. Security Controls

- **Technical:** TLS in transit; AES-256 at rest on managed laptops and servers; multi-factor authentication on all accounts; quarterly access reviews; immutable audit logs for matter management systems.
- **Organisational:** annual privacy training; clear-desk policy; visitor sign-in; locked filing for hard-copy matter bundles; need-to-know access principle enforced by case-management ACLs.
- **Incident response:** suspected breaches are reported to the DPO within 1 hour of discovery. The DPO assesses notifiability under Article 33 (ICO within 72 hours) and Article 34 (high-risk breaches to data subjects without undue delay).

## 5. International Transfers

Personal data is not transferred outside the UK except under a valid transfer mechanism: an adequacy regulation, the UK International Data Transfer Agreement (IDTA), or the EU SCCs combined with the UK Addendum. A Transfer Risk Assessment (TRA) is documented on the matter file before any restricted transfer.

## 6. Processors and Sub-processors

All third-party processors (e.g. cloud-storage providers, e-discovery vendors, expert witnesses) are engaged under written contracts containing the Article 28(3) controller-processor terms. The firm maintains a register of processors and audits high-risk processors annually.

On-premises AI systems used to process client data must run within the firm's network with no outbound connectivity to third-party AI providers. This includes the firm's deployment of locally-hosted large language models for document review, drafting assistance, and precedent search.

## 7. Records of Processing

The firm maintains an Article 30 Record of Processing Activities (ROPA) covering all processing categories: client matters, employment, business administration, marketing, and IT/security. The ROPA is reviewed quarterly by the DPO.

## 8. Retention

- Client files: 7 years from matter closure (defamation/personal-injury 7 years; commercial-property 15 years; trust files indefinitely).
- AML records: 5 years from end of business relationship.
- HR files: 6 years post-employment.
- Marketing-consent records: until consent withdrawn or 3 years of inactivity.
- Audit logs: 7 years (immutable, hash-chained).

After the retention period, records are securely deleted. Hard copies are shredded by an approved secure-destruction provider with certificates of destruction retained.

## 9. Training and Accountability

All staff complete annual data-protection refresher training. Failure to complete training within 30 days of due date triggers a temporary access restriction. Partners are personally accountable for data protection within their teams under Article 5(2) (the accountability principle).
